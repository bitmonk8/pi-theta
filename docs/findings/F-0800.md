## Finding

GOV-15's release-process goal is named with two different root words across the corpus. The owner page is titled `# Source-language stability`, lives at `governance/source-language-stability.md`, anchors the NFR aggregator bullet `<a id="source-language-stability">` labelled `**Source-language stability.**`, and is referenced from `glossary.md`, `language-and-architecture.md`, and `future-considerations/model-changes-and-non-goals.md` by that name. The rule's own normative title and prose, by contrast, consistently use *equivalence*: `**GOV-15 (loom 1.x source-language equivalence — release-process goal).**`, "the equivalence promise", "equivalent under observable (c)", and the ten-plus cross-page references in `diagnostics/`, `hard-ceilings/`, `req-id-prefix-table-active-b.md`, `req-id-prefix-table-retired.md`, `release-version-naming.md`, and the three plan files.

The two words are not synonyms. *Stability* names a release-over-release persistence posture (a goal at the release-process level); *equivalence* names the byte-identical-after-normalisation relation GOV-15's first paragraph actually defines as the operative observable. Today they happen to refer to the same rule, but a reader skimming for "what is the spec promising?" arrives at two different conceptual answers depending on which spelling they land on first. The practical cost is search and reviewer-attention: a `grep` on one form silently omits the other, and the duplication invites future drift where one spelling acquires a qualifier the other doesn't (e.g. the diagnostic-registry carve-out was added only to the "equivalence" prose; the bullet label still reads "Source-language stability").

Subsequent migrations preserved the split intact: `c253233` (2026-05-07) renamed GOV-13→GOV-15 keeping the "equivalence" wording in the rule body; `7995d3c` (2026-06-05) moved the rule body into a new owner page and named that page `source-language-stability.md` / `# Source-language stability` — choosing the orientation-bullet spelling for the file/heading while leaving the rule's normative title and prose on the "equivalence" spelling, cementing the two-name pattern. No commit ever attempted to unify them.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single
**State:** shaped

### Recommendation

Adopt *equivalence* everywhere. The dominant existing usage is already on "equivalence" (the rule title, the rule prose, ~30 cross-page references), the word matches the operative observable GOV-15 actually defines (a byte-identical-after-normalisation relation), and the rename eliminates the dual-naming surface in a single mechanical pass rather than asking every future maintainer to police it.

Rename the owner page to `source-language-equivalence.md` and re-anchor its H1 / aggregator-bullet anchor / aggregator-bullet label / all cross-page reference labels to use *equivalence*. Update the TOC entry on `governance.md`, the glossary entry, the `language-and-architecture.md` top-of-file comment, the `future-considerations/model-changes-and-non-goals.md` entry and `Recorded at:` line, the `req-id-prefix-table-retired.md` HTML comment, the two plan leaves `H5e`/`H6a`, and the two plan support files `conventions.md`/`coverage-matrix.md`. This is a single search-and-replace pass across the 13 spec files + 3 plan/plan-support files; the diagnostic-registry-carve-out language and per-page lock-step references all already use "equivalence" prose, so the asymmetry vanishes naturally.

Edge cases the implementer must watch: (i) leave a `<a id="source-language-stability"></a>` alias anchor on the renamed bullet so loom-1.x stale links keep resolving; (ii) the file path change `source-language-stability.md` → `source-language-equivalence.md` must update all relative-link targets in the 13 spec files and 4 plan files in the same commit per GOV-30 lock-step; (iii) the GOV-14 editorial note on `req-id-prefix-table-active-b.md`, the `req-id-prefix-table-retired.md` HTML-comment provenance record, and the glossary `GOV-N` entry already use "equivalence" wording and need only the link-target update; (iv) preserve the `#gov-15`, `#gov-15-loads-cleanly`, `#gov-15-fixture-suite`, `#ceiling-set-carve-out`, `#diagnostic-registry-carve-out`, `#attribution-test`, and `#operational-definitions` sub-anchors verbatim — they are the bulk of inbound link traffic and are spelling-neutral.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

Implementers can still produce a working system — runtime behaviour does not turn on the spelling. The cost is borne by reviewers and corpus maintainers: a search on one root word silently omits the other (across ≈15 spec files and 3 plan files), and the asymmetric distribution of qualifiers between the two spellings creates a slow-moving drift surface that future GOV-15 amendments must remember to keep in lock-step manually.
