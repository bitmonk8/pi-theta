## Finding

The version-bump inventory-closure audit specifies two distinct closed sets of five elements each, both numbered with the same parenthesised-ordinal notation on the same procedure surface. The *Failure-surface contract* (`audit-failures.md`) enumerates **five failure families** `(1)`â€“`(5)`; step 2(b) (`version-bump-step2b.md`) enumerates **five resolution branches** `(1)`â€“`(5)`. A contributor working a red audit reads tokens like `family (5)` and `branch (5)`, or `family (4)` and `branch (4)`, that look identical but name unrelated things.

The collision is actively misleading because the familyâ†’branch routing is *cross-indexed*, not diagonal: family (4) (out-of-scope import/access shape) routes to **branch (5)** (rewrite-shape), and family (5) (stale/malformed marker) routes to **branch (4)** (delete/rewrite marker). A reader who carries a bare `(N)` from one space into the other lands on the wrong resolution arm at exactly the two rows where the numbers cross. The same prose also stacks bare overlap notations â€” "(1)âˆ§(3) overlap", "(2)âˆ§(3) overlap" â€” that a reader must mentally tag as branch-space, not family-space.

This is purely a presentational defect. The normative routing table in `audit-failures.md` pins the familyâ†’branch mapping unambiguously, and the machine-readable surface is unaffected: the wire discriminator carries an implementation-chosen kebab-case `<family>` short name under the `audit/<class>/<family>/<symptom>` grammar, never the parenthesised number. The ordinals exist only to let humans cross-reference the spec prose, and that is the cross-reference they make hardest.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation
