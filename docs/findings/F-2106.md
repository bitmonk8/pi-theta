## Finding

`seam` is a coined term-of-art that the spec uses normatively on many pages, yet the glossary carries no headword for it. The glossary's own preamble states that an entry must be added "when the spec coins a new term that is reused on more than one page" — `seam` crosses that threshold decisively and is nonetheless absent.

The word is load-bearing in two distinct senses, and the glossary anchors neither:

- **Forward-compatibility seam** — a typed/structural extension point reserved in loom 1.0 so a deferred extension can land backward-compatibly. Each is pinned by a `> **loom 1.0 seam — <name>.**` blockquote on its owning page (e.g. the `Resolver` seam in `imports.md`, the named-argument-invocation / per-call-timeout / symlink-resolution-hardening seams in `invocation.md`). This sense is *counted*: the "12 typed/structural seams" inventory is a normative integer-count aggregator under GOV-31, and the seam-vs-non-goal distinction is itself a scope-classification axis.
- **Host-interface / dependency-injection seam** — a named injected interface that decouples the runtime from a host dependency and supplies the deterministic-test substrate (e.g. the `Checkpoint` seam in `cancellation.md`, and the `SchemaValidator` / `Clock` / `FileWatcher` / `FileSystem.readBytes` / `IdSource` seams in `pi-integration-contract/host-interfaces-services.md`). The `Checkpoint` seam is explicitly "purely a test surface"; a forward-compatibility seam is not.

A reader meeting "the `Checkpoint` seam" or "loom 1.0 reserves 12 … seams" has no anchor that explains what `seam` denotes, that two senses exist, or that they carry different obligations (one is a counted, version-governed extension point; the other is an internal injected interface). The intended meaning — a Feathers-style named indirection point — is non-obvious from the bare word. The glossary already admits comparably load-bearing coined terms (`type sink`, `binder`, `PIC`), so the omission is an inconsistency in glossary coverage, not a deliberate exclusion.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Add one alphabetised `seam` entry to `docs/spec_topics/glossary.md` that defines the term and distinguishes its two senses:

1. **Forward-compatibility seam** — a typed/structural extension point reserved in loom 1.0 so a deferred extension can be added backward-compatibly; each is pinned by a `> **loom 1.0 seam — <name>.**` blockquote on its owning page, and the inventory is counted under the GOV-31 integer-count aggregator. `See:` [Future Considerations — Surface extensions](./future-considerations/surface-extensions.md#surface-extensions-v1-leaves-a-seam) and [`spec.md` — Forward-compatibility seams](./overview-and-orientation.md#scope).
2. **Host-interface / dependency-injection seam** (a.k.a. injection or test seam) — a named injected interface that decouples the runtime from a host dependency and supplies the deterministic-test substrate; internal, not part of the loom-facing or extension-facing contract. `See:` [Pi Integration Contract — host interfaces](./pi-integration-contract/host-interfaces-services.md) (e.g. the `Checkpoint`, `SchemaValidator`, `Clock`, `FileWatcher`, `IdSource` seams).

State explicitly that the two senses are distinct (the forward-compatibility sense is version-counted and author-visible via its blockquote; the DI sense is internal and may be "purely a test surface"). Do not enumerate a fixed pair of "two seams" — there are 12 inventoried forward-compatibility seams plus several host-interface seams; the entry should reference the inventories by `See:` pointer rather than hardcode a count. Keep the entry descriptive and defer the normative contracts to the linked pages, per the glossary's stated convention.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

A reader cannot resolve `seam` to a definition and may conflate the two senses — treating a counted, version-governed forward-compatibility seam as if it were an internal test seam, or vice versa. The canonical pages still define each concrete contract, so a careful implementer can produce a working system; the cost is comprehension friction and a coverage inconsistency against the glossary's stated admission rule.
