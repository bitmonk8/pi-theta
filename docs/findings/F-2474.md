## Finding

The Diagnostic-emission-isolation paragraph and its Acceptance-criteria section repeatedly lean on the term-of-art **"leaf"** to draw the line between what the spec pins and what it delegates — e.g. "the exact serialisation primitive (e.g. `JSON.stringify`) is **leaf-owned** and not pinned here", "the **leaf-owned** serialisation-and-emission sequence" (PIC-25, PIC-28), and "the **leaf owns** fixture-shape; branch coverage and the byte-exact emission forms are normative" (Acceptance criteria). The same idiom recurs across the Pi-integration-contract pages (`drain-state-contract.md`, `tool-registration-lifetime.md`, `unknown-reason-rule.md`, `session-only-degraded-state.md`) and elsewhere in the corpus.

"leaf" is never defined anywhere in the spec corpus. There is no glossary entry for it, and no first-use gloss. Its meaning is the downstream **implementation-plan leaf** — the unit `docs/plan_topics/**` and the coverage matrix are organised around — which the spec corpus deliberately does not bind (GOV-18 *binding scope*: the corpus binds only the implementation target and the corpus itself, and may name downstream consumers only "in implementation-neutral terms … to anchor an obligation's purpose"). So "leaf-owned X" means "X is chosen by the implementation and is not pinned by the spec at this site." A reader who arrives at `diagnostic-emission-isolation.md` without that GOV-18 context cannot reliably distinguish that intended reading ("implementation-chosen / unconstrained by the spec") from the alternative ("constrained, but by some other lower-level document I must go find").

The contrast clauses that sit beside each use ("…and not pinned here, but the field names … MUST be preserved verbatim", "…the leaf owns fixture-shape; branch coverage … are normative") do make the operative spec-level obligation recoverable in each instance, so this does not by itself cause behavioural divergence. But a load-bearing term that gates the read of adjacent normative MUSTs, used corpus-wide, with no definition, is a standing clarity gap that should be closed with a single glossary anchor.

The defect — a normative-adjacent term-of-art with no glossary anchor — has therefore been present since the term first entered the corpus and was never subsequently defined.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Add a glossary entry in `docs/spec_topics/glossary.md` for **leaf** (with **leaf-owned** as a sub-form), defining it as the downstream implementation-plan unit that the spec corpus does not bind, and stating that "**leaf-owned**" marks a choice the implementation makes that the spec deliberately leaves unpinned at that site (in contrast to the normative MUSTs it sits beside). Cross-reference GOV-18 (binding scope) as the authority for why such choices fall outside the corpus's normative reach.

Edge cases for the author to keep consistent:

- Keep the definition implementation-neutral so it does not itself bind a third party (GOV-18 *internals-prohibition*): define "leaf" by its role ("the downstream unit of implementation work the spec's obligations are consumed by"), not by naming `plan_topics/**` file shapes or the coverage matrix's internals.
- The glossary entry covers all corpus uses at once (PIC pages, subagent prose, governance retirement notes); no per-site edit is required, so existing bare uses of "leaf-owned" can stay as-is once the anchor exists. If any site is genuinely ambiguous on its own, prefer the inline gloss "(implementation-chosen)" there rather than re-defining the term.
- This sits in the same family as the existing "seam" glossary gap; if both are fixed together, use one consistent entry style.

## Consequence

**Severity:** advisory

An implementer reading the diagnostic-emission isolation rules must infer from GOV-18 context that "leaf-owned" means "implementation-chosen, not spec-pinned." The adjacent "MUST … verbatim" / "… are normative" clauses keep the operative obligation recoverable, so two implementers do not diverge on behaviour; the cost is reader friction and a latent risk of misreading "leaf-owned" as "pinned elsewhere" rather than "unconstrained."
