## Finding

The binder-inference paragraph ends with: "the `pi.on("agent_end", …)` and session-local `subscribe` channels that the prompt-mode and subagent-mode drivers consume play no part in the binder call, and the **global-`agent_end` bans those drivers impose do not apply to it**." The bolded phrase is written as if it were naming an already-pinned, cross-referenced constraint — but no such "ban" is defined anywhere in the spec under that name, and the sentence carries no link.

The two prohibitions the sentence is actually pointing at do exist and are normative:

- Prompt-mode *Error detection* (`docs/spec_topics/pi-integration-contract/conversation-drive.md`, anchor `#prompt-mode-error-detection`): "The runtime MUST NOT subscribe to the global `pi.on("agent_end", …)` event for query completion: that event fires for every `AgentSession` in the process … with no per-session origin marker, so a global handler cross-fires across concurrent looms or sibling subagents and resolves the wait on the wrong turn."
- Subagent **PIC-42** (`docs/spec_topics/pi-integration-contract/subagent.md`, anchor `#pic-42`, "Session-local `subscribe`, not global `pi.on`"): "The runtime MUST use the session-local `subscribe` API rather than the global `pi.on("agent_end", …)` event for the same per-session-scoping reason given for prompt mode."

A reader of binder-inference.md who tries to follow the "bans" reference finds nothing — neither a defined term nor a cross-reference — and has to reconstruct from context that "those drivers" means prompt-mode and subagent-mode and that the "ban" is the prohibition against using global `pi.on("agent_end", …)` as a query-completion signal. The defect is purely editorial (the constraint being referenced is sound and normatively defined elsewhere), but the wording invents a term ("bans") that appears nowhere else and elides the cross-reference that would let the reader confirm it.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Rewrite the trailing clause of the second paragraph of `binder-inference.md` to (a) drop the coined word "bans", (b) name the constraint being referenced, and (c) link both pinning sites. Replace:

> …the `pi.on("agent_end", …)` and session-local `subscribe` channels that the prompt-mode and subagent-mode drivers consume play no part in the binder call, and the global-`agent_end` bans those drivers impose do not apply to it.

with something equivalent to:

> …the `pi.on("agent_end", …)` and session-local `subscribe` channels that the prompt-mode and subagent-mode drivers consume play no part in the binder call, so the prohibitions those drivers carry against using `pi.on("agent_end", …)` as a query-completion signal — pinned for prompt mode at [Conversation drive — Error detection](./conversation-drive.md#prompt-mode-error-detection) and for subagent mode at [PIC-42 — Session-local `subscribe`, not global `pi.on`](./subagent.md#pic-42) — have no analogue here.

Edge cases for the implementer:

- Keep the binder's own positive disposition ("Its terminal signal is therefore the resolution (or rejection) of that promise, not an `agent_end` event") intact; only the trailing clause needs replacing.
- Use the existing relative-path / anchor form already used elsewhere in `binder-inference.md` (`./conversation-drive.md#…`, `./subagent.md#…`); don't introduce a new linking style.
- Do not invent a new term for the prohibition. The two pinning sites already state it as a `MUST NOT` against using the global event for query completion; the binder paragraph should reference that, not rename it.

## Consequence

**Severity:** cosmetic

A reader cannot verify the asserted exception without first locating the referenced prohibition by manual search of two sibling pages. Implementation behaviour is not at risk — the binder call is already pinned to resolve off `Promise<AssistantMessage>` rather than off `agent_end`, and the prompt-mode / subagent-mode bans are clearly normative on their own pages — but the sentence advertises a defined concept ("the global-`agent_end` bans") that the corpus does not define under that name.
