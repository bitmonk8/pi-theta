## Finding

The inventory-closure audit resolves three carrier categories against a single `SDK_SURFACE_INVENTORY`: category (2) named imports by **leftmost**-dot-separated-`path`-segment match, and categories (1) (`pi.<member>`) and (3) (`ctx.<member>` on `ExtensionContext` / `ExtensionCommandContext`) by **rightmost**-segment match (the *Category-(1)/(3) inventory join key (per-category specialisation)* in `audit-resolution.md`). The category-(1)/(3) rule matches `<member>` against "the rightmost dot-separated segment of **an entry's** `path` field" with no restriction on the entry's `kind`. Because each audit-participating entry kind belongs to a specific carrier category (the existing `namespace-function` and the new category-(1) kind are `pi`-rooted; the new category-(3) kind is `ctx`-rooted), an unscoped rightmost-segment match lets a `pi.<member>` access resolve against a category-(3) `ctx.*` entry whose member name happens to coincide, and a `ctx.<member>` access resolve against a `pi.*` entry — a member name shared across the two carriers (e.g. a `shutdown` declared on both surfaces) resolves to whichever entry's rightmost segment matches first, with no carrier-category guard. Two implementers will disambiguate this differently (lex-min tie-break, walk order, or carrier-aware filtering), so the audit's resolution is not deterministic as specified.

The same omission corrupts the uniqueness rule. The *Category-(2) inventory join key* paragraph extends rightmost-segment uniqueness across categories: "For category-(1) and category-(3) audit-participating kinds, the same uniqueness rule applies symmetrically on the rightmost dot-separated segment … `literal-read/duplicate-rightmost-segment`." Applied cross-category, a legitimate category-(1) entry `pi.shutdown` and a legitimate category-(3) entry `ctx.shutdown` (or `ExtensionContext.shutdown`) share the rightmost segment `shutdown` and collide, so the literal-read assertion forbids two genuinely distinct members on two different carriers — a false-positive audit failure that blocks a correct inventory.

Finally, the category-(3) resolution prose in `audit-target-categories.md` cross-references the wrong join key: "a member … resolves against any `SDK_SURFACE_INVENTORY` category-(3) entry whose `path` field matches the access per the [Category-(2) inventory join key](./audit-resolution.md#audit-category-2-inventory-join-key) below (generalised to category (3) per this paragraph)". That anchor is the **leftmost**-segment rule; category (3) is governed by the rightmost-segment *Category-(1)/(3) inventory join key* section (anchor `#audit-category-1-3-inventory-join-key`), which the page does not point at. The "generalised to category (3) per this paragraph" clause is also stale: the generalisation is owned by the dedicated category-(1)/(3) section, not by the *Target surface categories* paragraph. (The minimum-entry-shape paragraph on the same page exhibits the milder form of the same mis-pointing: its body text correctly states "categories (1) and (3) use rightmost-segment matching" but its hyperlink still targets the category-(2) anchor.)

All three are facets of one defect: the category-(1)/(3) join-key specialisation was authored without a carrier-category guard, and the upstream cross-reference was never repointed at it.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

Tag each audit-participating `SDK_SURFACE_INVENTORY` entry kind with the carrier category it belongs to (category 1 for the `pi`-rooted kinds — the existing `namespace-function` and the new non-capability category-(1) kind; category 3 for the new `ctx`-rooted category-(3) kind), and scope both the resolution match and the uniqueness rule by that category:

- In the *Category-(1)/(3) inventory join key* paragraph (`audit-resolution.md`), state that a category-(1) `pi.<member>` access resolves by rightmost-segment match **only against entries whose kind belongs to category (1)**, and a category-(3) `ctx.<member>` access resolves by rightmost-segment match **only against entries whose kind belongs to category (3)**. An access whose member matches the rightmost segment of an entry in the *other* category MUST NOT resolve against it.
- In the *Category-(2) inventory join key* paragraph (`audit-resolution.md`), make the rightmost-segment `duplicate-rightmost-segment` uniqueness rule **per-category**: two entries collide only when they share both the rightmost segment and the carrier category. A category-(1) `pi.shutdown` and a category-(3) `ctx.shutdown` are legal and MUST NOT fire `literal-read/duplicate-rightmost-segment`.
- In the *Target surface categories* category-(3) prose (`audit-target-categories.md`), repoint the cross-reference from `#audit-category-2-inventory-join-key` to `#audit-category-1-3-inventory-join-key`, and drop the stale "(generalised to category (3) per this paragraph)" clause (the rightmost-segment generalisation is owned by the category-(1)/(3) section). Repoint the minimum-entry-shape paragraph's hyperlink to the same anchor so its link target matches its already-correct body text.

The entry `kind` discriminator the minimum-entry-shape obligation already pins is the natural scope key; no new field is required beyond a normative statement of which kinds map to which category (the implementation already owns the literal kind names). Edge case for the implementer: the lex-min tie-break for legal multi-segment matches remains, but now operates within the per-category candidate set rather than across all entries.

## Consequence

**Severity:** correctness

As written, two conforming audit implementations diverge: an unscoped rightmost-segment match resolves a `pi.<member>` access against a category-(3) `ctx.*` entry (and vice versa) whenever a member name is shared across carriers, and the cross-category `duplicate-rightmost-segment` uniqueness rule fires a false `literal-read` failure that forbids a legitimate inventory carrying the same member name on both `pi` and `ctx`. The audit is post-1.0 hardening rather than a loom-1.0 merge gate, so the impact is a non-deterministic / spuriously-failing audit if and when it is published, not a 1.0 implementation blocker.
