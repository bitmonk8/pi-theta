## Finding

The bounded-walk rule in [DISC-6](../../../docs/spec_topics/discovery/package-and-settings.md) caps the walk at `looms.scanPackagesMaxFiles` files "inspected", and separately defines a per-read deadline whose timeout path *abandons* the read and continues with the next candidate. The two rules touch the same counter but the spec never says how they compose: when a candidate's `package.json` read fires its per-read deadline, does that candidate count toward `scanPackagesMaxFiles` or not?

The verb "inspected" reads natively two ways. Read as *attempted*, each candidate consumes one slot whether or not the read settled cleanly; read as *successfully parsed* (the surrounding paragraphs use "inspect" of the `package.json` parse outcome), a timed-out candidate is dropped from the count and the walk can issue arbitrarily many further reads before tripping the file-count cap. The two implementations have different observable cap-trip points: 2000 successive timeouts exhaust the cap under the first reading; under the second they do not, and the walk is bounded only by `scanPackagesTimeoutMs` in that pathological branch.

The conformance corollary is that the `cap` field on a `loom/load/discovery-slow` warning fired during a partially-slow walk is not a function of inputs — two implementers wiring the counter differently both pass the existing `V10b` tests.

**History:** 475155c first wrote the bounded-walk rule with the verb "inspected" as the counter for `looms.scanPackagesMaxFiles`, at a point in the corpus when every candidate either parsed (counted) or hit a synchronous filesystem failure (not counted). 25e8a60 then added the per-read deadline and the abandonment / `package-read-timeout` path, introducing a third candidate outcome that "inspected" had no rule for. No subsequent revision (through the current head at `443ee4e`) has reconciled the counter against that third outcome, so the silence is the residue of the two changes layering.

## Solution Space

**Shape:** single

### Recommendation

State that a candidate whose per-read deadline fires **does** count toward `looms.scanPackagesMaxFiles` — each attempted `package.json` read consumes one slot, regardless of whether the read settled, timed out, or failed at the filesystem layer. Add one sentence to the DISC-6 paragraph in `docs/spec_topics/discovery/package-and-settings.md`, sited next to the existing "On per-read timeout the in-flight read is abandoned …" clause, of the shape:

> A timed-out read counts toward `looms.scanPackagesMaxFiles` (the cap-check site treats each attempted candidate read as one inspected file, regardless of whether the read returned, timed out, or failed at the filesystem layer).

This reading matches the operator-facing intent of the cap (bound total work, including pathological slow roots), keeps `maxFiles` as an honest upper bound on the number of candidate `package.json` reads the walk issues per session, and is the only reading that prevents a single all-slow root from issuing arbitrarily many further reads before `scanPackagesTimeoutMs` independently trips. Implementer edge case: the increment happens at the same cap-check site that handles parse-success and stat-failure outcomes (no separate counter path for the timeout branch), so the existing simultaneous-cap tie-break rule (file-count predicate before time predicate) is unaffected.

Mirror the new sentence in the `V10b-T` / `V10b` **Tests.** bullets by adding a citing test: with `FakeClock`-driven per-read timeouts on every candidate, the walk trips `loom/load/discovery-slow` with `cap: "looms.scanPackagesMaxFiles"` after exactly `scanPackagesMaxFiles` candidates rather than continuing past it.

## Consequence

**Severity:** correctness

Two reasonable implementations diverge on the same `package.json` corpus: one trips `loom/load/discovery-slow` with `cap: "looms.scanPackagesMaxFiles"` on a slow root, the other never does. A conformance test that asserts the cap-trip point on a corpus with timed-out reads is non-determinate against the spec.
